Chromatography

A Quantified Visualization of Movies

By Kevin Lim, Hyeon-su Kang, Jennifer He

Abstract

We explore ways of studying film through a quantified way and use information visualization so that the film maker or critic can compare and contrast multiple films at the same time. We gather data about the film in three main ways: extracting color palette, parsing the script, and visualizing the audio. This visualized data is then used to make macro trends of film making clearer and also start a discussion of a field of quantified film studies.

Overview

Based on my interests in movies, I proposed that we explore the data in film, by means of information visualization. No single person can process and retain all the information in multiple—or even a single—films at once due to the density of data in a film and analyzing meaningful comparisons is that much harder. Thus the challenge we decided to take on were first quantifying film data into more abstract information and visualizing it in a way that's more meaningful to the artist.

We explored several methods, but the one I'll focus on in this page is the particular method I worked on: Color Palette visualization. Below are a few examples, but one can also try out our interactive iPython Notebook Prototype

The main way of contextualizing abstract information—we hypothesized— would be through comparisons that place the data in a surrounding context. One such way was through enabling comparisons across genres of films. Here we see that the action films we mined have palettes concentrated on the darker, lower values versus comedy which seem to use higher value, and even more staurated palettes.

Another comparison we wanted to see were how color palette uses varied across decades. Film has built up enough history that each period has its "looks" or "styles" that were decided partly by technology and artist direction. Such a visualization we conjectured would be of great interest to the film historian and critic.

Future Work

We would like to continue this work by gathering more film data and therefore making our dataset more dense and representative, without the selection bias of our team. Further, an actual interactive application will go a long way of users finding their own purpose and need.
